# Open Question 001: Can you align incentives without coercion?

*Status: open. Started 2026-06-30.*

## The question

Most durable coordination in human history runs on some mix of incentive and force. A robust utopia needs people to mostly do prosocial things at scale, reliably, across generations. How much of that can come from well-designed incentives, voluntary structure, and good defaults, and how much ultimately leans on coercion that we have just stopped noticing?

## Why it matters

If the honest answer is "you always need a big stick somewhere," then a lot of utopian designs are quietly authoritarian and should be evaluated as such. If the answer is "more than we think can be voluntary," then the design work is about finding those mechanisms and stress-testing them.

## What would move my view

- Concrete examples of large-scale, long-lived coordination that ran mostly on voluntary buy-in (and what propped them up).
- Mechanisms that make defection expensive without a central enforcer (reputation, federation, exit rights, mutual insurance).
- Honest accounting of where each of those quietly relies on an enforcer anyway.

## Early notes

- "No coercion" and "no enforcement" are different claims. Rules people opted into, with a real right of exit, are not obviously coercive.
- Exit rights may be the load-bearing piece. Voice plus exit changes the whole game versus voice alone.
- Watch for hidden coercion in defaults and lock-in, not just in laws.
