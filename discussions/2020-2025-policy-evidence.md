# Discussion: Policy Evidence (raising standard of living)

*Grew out of the Utopia Nation 2020 game (Eunomia / California). What the evidence actually says about the levers, and how to apply them. Started 2026-06-30. Companion to `../principles/behavioral-design.md`.*

## Why aren't known high-ROI interventions already deployed?

It is almost never a knowledge problem. The interventions are known; the bottleneck is **money, state capacity / corruption, political economy (vested interests), and time.** A benevolent-dictator game hands you legitimacy + a clean competent state for free , which is precisely the hardest part of real development. The lesson for real-world utopia: *the binding constraint on flourishing is usually institutions and implementation, not ideas.*

## Fare gates: gate trains, free buses , and when it generalizes

- **Rail justifies gates:** enclosed, meaningful farebox recovery, and the gate doubles as access control / order (most of the BART safety story , though BART's new-gate results are early and partly perception-based).
- **Buses justify free:** fares barely dent operating costs, collection slows boarding (real dwell-time cost), enforcement friction is high. Free-bus pilots (Boston, Kansas City, Tallinn) show faster boarding + ridership for trivial revenue loss.
- **Global generalization is partial.** Rail gates generalize to high-ridership metros worldwide, but the *order/safety* rationale is contingent on a high local disorder baseline (salient in the US, not in Tokyo). Free buses generalize only to *subsidized public* systems , they break where private/informal operators live entirely on fares (matatus, jeepneys) with no subsidy to replace them.
- **The real rule:** gate where farebox recovery is high and disorder is a real problem; free where fares are trivial and collection causes friction, *provided* there is subsidy to replace lost revenue. The substantive transit lever is frequency / coverage / speed (access to jobs and care), not fare enforcement.

## Cutting crime without restricting rights

The central insight: **the most effective crime interventions are the least rights-restricting; the rights-costly tools are mostly the low-evidence ones.**

- **Tier 1 (strong evidence, ~zero rights cost):** greening vacant lots (Philadelphia RCT , cheap clean-and-green: haul trash, grass + trees, low fence, mow; ~$1,600/lot; cut gun violence), street lighting (NYC RCT), lead abatement, **CBT for high-risk youth** (Chicago *Becoming a Man*, Liberia STYL , teaches noticing/slowing automatic reactive responses; ~45% violent-arrest drops), summer/youth jobs, addiction (MAT) + mental-health treatment + Housing First, alcohol pricing/density, early childhood, cash/poverty reduction.
- **Tier 2 (strong evidence, *targeted*, modest rights cost):** hot-spots policing (places, not people), focused deterrence (Boston Ceasefire , the few driving violence, services + a due-process warning), certainty-over-severity (which means *less* incarceration).
- **Tier 3 (high rights cost, weak/counterproductive , avoid):** mass stop-and-frisk, broad surveillance, mass incarceration / long sentences, sweeping broken-windows misdemeanor crackdowns. The US over-relies on this tier.
- Because the effective frontier and the rights-respecting frontier mostly overlap, you can cut crime *and* expand liberty.

## Concrete: the graduated-justice model (Marcus, 15, shoplifting)

Apply behavioral design (`../principles/behavioral-design.md`): certainty from offense one, proportionate escalation, root-cause treatment, keep him out of the criminogenic deep end (record, incarceration) as long as possible.

- **First offense:** immediate but *diversionary* , no criminal record (formal processing increases reoffending); restitution + a restorative conversation + a screening for the driver (hungry? connect to food assistance); a mentor relationship begins.
- **Second offense:** escalate consequence *and* support , heavier accountability + enrollment in CBT + a paid youth-job placement (kills the economic/boredom driver), deeper mentorship; still out of the formal record if possible.
- **Third offense:** firm but rehabilitative , swift-certain-*modest* sanctions (HOPE model: a quick short consequence, not years), continued CBT + job + a family intervention; secure placement only as a genuine last resort for serious/violent persistence.
- **Why this beats "10 strikes -> 20 years":** that design loses early certainty (so no deterrence and the behavior entrenches) and then drops a disproportionate, criminogenic, rights-costly cliff. The evidence-based inverse is certain-and-escalating from offense one, with support, never reaching a 20-year cliff for property crime. Keep the humane "give chances / don't ruin his life early" instinct; add early certainty; drop the cliff.

## What a more aggressive, evidence-maxxed California would add

- **A fentanyl/overdose blitz (MAT + naloxone saturation + treatment-on-demand)** , the single biggest missing near-term QALY lever (~10,000+ CA overdose deaths/yr, disproportionately young).
- **Housing , go even harder** (end single-family-only zoning, social housing at scale; supply lowers rents).
- **Vision Zero road redesign** (~4,000 CA road deaths/yr; engineering-only fixes).
- **Mental-health scale-up + Housing First.**
- **A state child allowance** (the 2021 CTC cut child poverty fast).
- **Reallocate the $25B** from frozen-tech equities (no breakthroughs to capture in a static-tech world) into the above.
